About me

Three words:
Curious. Learner.
Adapter.

Curiosity is what first led me into computer science. Learning is what keeps me here. At this point I've accepted it's just who I am.

I enjoy working on challenging problems — especially in environments where there's pressure to think clearly and build meaningful solutions. Those situations tend to bring out my best work. Over time, this mindset has helped me stay disciplined and push myself to keep improving. Keyword: keep improving. I'm not done yet.

My primary interests lie in AI, Data Science, ML, and Deep Learning. What excites me most is building systems that start as an idea and gradually evolve into something useful and well-engineered. My goal when working on a project is simple: the final product should satisfy me first as a creator. Only when I feel it's meaningful, well-designed, and complete do I consider the work truly finished.

Beyond tech, I have a genuine interest in storytelling and creative expression. I enjoy crafting narratives and presenting ideas in ways that engage people. Many have appreciated my ability to explain complex concepts clearly — and honestly, that skill has gotten me further than any algorithm I've learned.

In the long term, I want to combine everything I'm learning — AI, innovation, storytelling, system building — to pursue entrepreneurial ventures where all of it comes together. I also enjoy taking initiative and helping teammates. Collaboration, for me, is about creating an environment where everyone can contribute their best. Growth through challenges isn't a tagline. It's just how I've moved through this field from day one.

Philosophy

How I think.
Why I win.

I believe good software should do more than just work — it should be understandable, meaningful, and well-designed. I think deeply about the problem before touching the keyboard. Most people skip this step. I think that's why most software is mediocre. And mediocre is not something I do.
01
I play to win. In hackathons, in chess, in code reviews, in conversations. Not to dominate — but because second place means I left something on the table. And I physically cannot accept that.
02
Principles aren't flexible. My standards for quality, punctuality, and integrity are non-negotiable — same way production code isn't negotiable. I don't ship broken. In code or in character.
03
The debugging arc is real. First you write the code confidently. Then it breaks. Then you google the error. Then you realise you googled the wrong thing. Then 2 hours later you find a missing semicolon. Then you understand the system better than anyone. That's the process. I've made peace with it.
04
The universe has been suspiciously kind. Semi-finalist at two major events. A CGPA that actually represents effort. Projects that work in production. I don't take credit for all of it — but I also don't think it's entirely coincidence. Let's just say the alignment is real.

Most CSE portfolios look identical. Mine doesn't — not because I'm trying to be different, but because I actually am different. There's a gap there.

I'm competitive in the way that matters: I don't need to see you lose, I need to see myself improve. In sports, chess, hackathons — I show up wanting to win. Sometimes I do. When I don't, I figure out exactly why, and that's usually more valuable anyway.

I also take the spiritual side of life seriously — not in an abstract way, but in the sense that discipline and intention seem to return results I can't fully explain. I've had enough timely wins to stay grateful and keep going. That's the whole philosophy really.
